  • The Big Bang

    Origin: The Big Bang Theory proposes that the universe began as an extremely hot and dense singularity about 13.8 billion years ago.The universe emerged from a "primeval atom".
    Formation of Elements: As the universe continued to expand and cool, the first atoms formed, primarily hydrogen and helium. Over time, these atoms clumped together under gravity to form stars and galaxies.
    Expansion of the Universe: Observations of distant galaxies show that they are moving away from us, and the farther away they are, the faster they are moving. This implies that the universe is expanding, consistent with the predictions of the Big Bang Theory.

  • Black Hole

    Black holes are fascinating cosmic phenomena. They're regions in space where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape from them once it crosses the event horizon. They're formed when massive stars collapse under their own gravity at the end of their life cycles.
    At the center of a black hole lies a singularity, where matter is infinitely dense and space-time is infinitely curved. Surrounding the singularity is the event horizon, the boundary beyond which nothing can return.
    Black holes come in different sizes, from stellar-mass black holes, which are several times more massive than the Sun.

  • Gravitational Waves

    Gravitational waves are ripples in the fabric of spacetime itself, caused by some of the most violent and energetic processes in the universe. They were predicted by Albert Einstein in his theory of general relativity in 1915 and were directly detected for the first time in 2015 by the Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory (LIGO). Gravitational waves are incredibly faint by the time they reach Earth, making them extremely difficult to detect. To do so, scientists use extremely sensitive instruments known as interferometers, which can measure tiny changes in the lengths of two perpendicular arms caused by passing gravitational waves.
